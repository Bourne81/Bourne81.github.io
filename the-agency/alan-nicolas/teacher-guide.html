// 01

What We're Trying to Identify

The assessment gives you Alan's self-reported data and MCQ performance. The class gives you live production data — the stuff MCQs can't measure. Your job in Session 01 is to listen, note, and map what you hear to teaching priorities. Every question you ask him, every task you give him, is a diagnostic instrument.

The five things you need to know by the end of Session 01:

1. Transfer interference
Which specific Brazilian Portuguese patterns are bleeding into his English? These are systematic — they will repeat every session until you name them and drill against them.
2. Confidence gap
Where does his language collapse under pressure? Is it vocabulary, structure, or does he simply shut down? The gap between his thinking and his English is the programme.
3. Register range
Can he shift from casual English to investor English to interview English? Which registers does he avoid? The one he avoids most is the one he needs most.
4. Avoidance patterns
What structures or vocabulary does he never attempt? Avoidance is diagnostic gold — it tells you where he's already aware of a weakness and compensating around it.
5. Natural rhythm
What is his default sentence length, pace, and filler style in English? This is his starting point. Everything you teach needs to be scaffolded from this baseline.
6. Written vs spoken gap
Compare his writing sample (from the assessment) to his spoken production. Many learners write far better than they speak — the gap tells you where to focus first.
// 02

Brazilian Interference Checklist

These are the most common Brazilian Portuguese → English transfer errors at B1–C1 level. Tick each one you hear during the session. The most frequently occurring patterns become Priority 1 items in the programme.

// HIGH PRIORITY — very common at B1–B2
1. Present perfect vs simple past
Brazilian Portuguese uses the simple past where English uses the present perfect. Result: "I did it yesterday" ✓ but also "I worked here for three years" (when still working) ✗.
Listen for: "I started my company in 2019 and I built it since then." → should be "have been building"
// HIGH PRIORITY — systematic error
2. Subject omission
Portuguese is a pro-drop language — the subject is implied. English requires it always. Result: "Is difficult to explain" / "Was the right decision".
Listen for: "Is hard to describe, but basically is a platform that..."
// HIGH PRIORITY — affects professional credibility
3. False cognates (the lethal ones)
actually (used for Portuguese "atualmente" = currently/nowadays) — "I actually work with AI" meaning "I currently work with AI". In English "actually" signals a correction.
eventually (used for Portuguese "eventualmente" = possibly/sometimes) — "Eventually we do conferences" meaning "sometimes we do conferences".
pretend (used for "pretender" = to intend) — "I pretend to expand globally" meaning "I intend to expand globally".
Note every false cognate use — they will damage credibility in investor contexts
// HIGH PRIORITY — grammar
4. Uncountable nouns treated as countable
Portuguese speakers regularly pluralise: "informations", "advices", "feedbacks", "knowledges", "researches". These are all uncountable in English.
Listen for: "I have many feedbacks from students" / "We do many researches"
// MEDIUM PRIORITY — affects fluency
5. Gerund vs infinitive after certain verbs
"enjoy to work" → "enjoy working" / "avoid to use" → "avoid using" / "suggest to do" → "suggest doing"
Listen for: "I enjoy to speak English" / "I avoid to make mistakes"
// MEDIUM PRIORITY — preposition transfer
6. Preposition errors
"in Monday" → "on Monday" / "in the morning of Monday" → common compound confusion / "explain me" → "explain to me" / "depend of" → "depend on"
// MEDIUM PRIORITY — syntax
7. Indirect question word order
"Can you tell me where is the meeting?" → "Can you tell me where the meeting is?" — Portuguese syntax inverts the verb; English indirect questions do not.
// MEDIUM PRIORITY — register
8. Make vs do confusion
"make a decision" ✓ / "do a decision" ✗ — "make a course" → "take a course" / "make an effort" ✓ / "do an effort" ✗
// WATCH FOR — discourse structure
9. Portuguese sentence structure imposed on English
Long subordinate clauses stacked in Portuguese order before the main point. In English, the main point leads. Listen for heavily front-loaded sentences that bury the key idea.
Portuguese pattern: "Because of the fact that we had already developed the technology, and given that the market was ready for it, we decided to launch."
English pattern: "We launched — the tech was ready and the market was too."
// WATCH FOR — pronunciation
10. Phonological transfer
Key sounds to listen for:
• th → often /d/ or /t/ ("dis" for "this", "tree" for "three")
• Final consonant clusters → "tests" → "tes-tis", "months" → simplification
• Vowel reduction → "economy" stress misplaced
• -ed past tense → /ɪd/ added where not needed ("worked" → "worked-id")
• Nasal vowels → English vowels nasalised unnecessarily
// 03

Live Elicitation Questions

These questions look like conversation but are structured to extract specific data. Ask naturally, in sequence, noting what you hear. Each question is designed to elicit a different aspect of his production.

  • // spontaneous production — fluency baseline "Tell me what you're working on right now — the most important thing on your plate today."
    Diagnostic: You get unprepared, topic-specific English. Note: sentence length, fillers ("like", "basically", "you know"), tense consistency, subject omission.
  • // narrative production — past tense / present perfect "Walk me through how Academia Lendária started — from the very beginning. What actually happened?"
    Diagnostic: Forces a past-tense narrative. Listen for: present perfect vs simple past confusion, false cognate "actually", verb sequence errors. Does he use "I started" or "I have started"?
  • // pressure response — directness test "If I told you right now that your English sounds like someone who learned from textbooks, not from life — what would you say?"
    Diagnostic: High pressure response. Does his English deteriorate? Does he hedge or stay direct? Note: conditional structures, vocabulary reach, confidence markers.
  • // register shift — investor register "Pretend I've never heard of AIOX. Pitch it to me in 60 seconds — I'm a US-based edtech investor."
    Diagnostic: Forces investor register. Does he shift register from casual? Note: precision of language, structure of argument, use of numbers and specifics, pronunciation under performance pressure.
  • // meta-awareness — self-diagnosis "When you imagine yourself speaking perfect English — the version you want — what sounds different from what you do now?"
    Diagnostic: Reveals what Alan already knows about his gaps. Self-awareness is teaching gold — it tells you what he'll be motivated to fix. Also forces him to use hedging, conditional language, and abstract vocabulary.
  • // vocabulary probe — range test "Describe the difference between AIOX and a regular online course platform — but without using the words 'content', 'course', or 'platform'."
    Diagnostic: Vocabulary constraints force precision. Can he find synonyms and paraphrases? Does he freeze or adapt? This reveals his active vocabulary range beyond stock phrases.
  • // written vs spoken gap "Read me what you wrote in the assessment — out loud, slowly, as if you were presenting it."
    Diagnostic: Compares his written and spoken English. Reveals pronunciation issues on his own words and whether he sounds as good aloud as on paper. Also listen for whether he auto-corrects while reading.
  • // interference trap — false cognates "What do you eventually want to do with AIOX internationally — what actually comes next?"
    Diagnostic: Uses "eventually" and "actually" naturally in the question. Does he echo them back with the wrong meaning? A positive sign if he uses them correctly unprompted. A flag if he mirrors the usage incorrectly.
  • // discourse structure — argument building "Make the case for why AI education will be the most important category in global edtech by 2030. You have 90 seconds."
    Diagnostic: Extended argument production. Does he structure: claim → evidence → conclusion? Or does he list points without connecting them? This reveals his English discourse logic vs Portuguese discourse logic.
  • // emotional register — personal language "What do you want people to feel when they finish a course on your platform?"
    Diagnostic: Forces emotional/abstract vocabulary — the hardest register for technical founders. Note: does he reach for specific words or retreat to generics ("good", "happy", "better")?
// 04

Spoken Task Analysis Guide

The assessment contains two recorded spoken tasks. Before Session 01, listen to both recordings with the checklist below. Use what you find to calibrate which live questions to lead with.

// spoken I — spontaneous production (60 seconds on AIOX + Academia Lendária)
What to listen for
□ How long is his first sentence before he pauses?
□ Does he use filler phrases? (basically, like, you know, kind of, so...)
□ How quickly does he get to the main point? (Portuguese speakers often delay)
□ Does he use present tense for past facts? ("We have 20,000 students" → past or current?)
□ Are there false cognate moments?
□ Does pronunciation clarity drop on longer words?
□ How does he end? Confident landing or trailing off?
// spoken II — pressure response (responding to "isn't that just ChatGPT?")
What to listen for
□ Does his sentence structure change under pressure? (Does he get shorter and choppier?)
□ Does he use hedging ("I think maybe..." vs "No — here's why...")?
□ Does his vocabulary precision improve or decline under challenge?
□ Is his argument structured (claim → rebuttal → evidence) or reactive (emotional, scattered)?
□ Does he finish strong or trail off?
□ How is his pronunciation under emotional engagement?

// 06

DISC-Informed Approach

Alan's DISC profile (from the assessment) determines how to deliver corrections and new material. Different DISC profiles respond very differently to the same teaching technique.

If his primary DISC is How to frame corrections What to avoid
D — Dominance Direct and fast. "That was wrong — here's the right one. Again." No softening. He respects precision and speed. Give him the pattern, then let him drill it to mastery himself. Over-explaining. He doesn't need the theory — he needs the result. Don't ask "how do you feel about the correction?"
I — Influence Frame errors as performance upgrades. "The way you said that now is B2. This version is C1 — it's more you." He responds to the idea of the best version of himself. Dry repetition without narrative. He needs the story of why this matters, not just the drill.
S — Steadiness Predictable structure, consistent feedback. Build slowly. He will improve steadily if the environment feels safe. Name errors gently — he's self-aware and already knows. Surprise challenges, sudden high-pressure tasks without warning. Ease into the pressure drills.
C — Conscientiousness Evidence-based. Show him the rule, the exception, the data. He will want to understand why before he accepts the correction. Give him reference material. Vague feedback. "That wasn't quite right" is useless to him. He needs "that was X, the correct form is Y because Z."
// 07

Suggested Session 01 Flow

// 00:00–05:00 — establish baseline
Open with question 1 (what's on his plate today)
Don't correct yet. Just listen. You're calibrating his natural baseline — sentence length, filler patterns, default tense. Take notes only, say nothing about what you're observing.
// 05:00–15:00 — narrative elicitation
Questions 2 and 5 (origin story + self-diagnosis)
The origin story forces past tense. The self-diagnosis reveals what he already knows about himself — this tells you what he's motivated to fix. Start introducing light corrections here: echo the correct form back in your response without flagging it explicitly.
// 15:00–30:00 — pressure tasks
Questions 3 and 4 (pressure response + investor pitch)
This is where you'll get the most diagnostic data. Push back. Ask follow-up questions that weren't prepared. Note exactly where the language breaks down — this is his ceiling for now. Tell him what you observed: "When I pushed back, you switched to shorter sentences and dropped the conditional structure. That's the work."
// 30:00–45:00 — register and vocabulary range
Questions 6 and 9 (vocabulary constraint + argument building)
The vocabulary constraint task (without the words "content", "course", "platform") is often the most revealing. Does he freeze or adapt? The argument task shows you his discourse logic in English. Compare it to how he'd naturally structure it in Portuguese.
// 45:00–55:00 — read-back and debrief
Question 7 (read the written sample aloud) + your observations
Have him read his written assessment response out loud. Then share your top 3 observations from the session — direct, specific, without softening. End with: "These three things are your first 90 days. Everything else comes after."
